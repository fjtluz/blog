Out of the silent planet

by C. S. Lewis

In "Out of the silent planet", the reader explores the wonders involved in the discovery a new world: the fascination with the an unexplored culture and ambient, but also the strangeness in overcoming the barrier of mistrust between different species. The book provides a rich cast of different species and their cultures, but also accentuating the differences (or lack there of) between the unknown species of Malacandra and the humans from Earth.

The book has a great emphasis on the religious aspect of the people of Malacandra. It also has shy but present references to Christianity - explaining that their religion is not distinct from our own. What the book does not do, however, is to try and evangelize the reader; any religious mentions are always low-profile, rarely name-dropping literal references to the gospel.

Another point of praise is regarding the topic of space colonization. Unlike Asimov's of Frank Herbert's approach, C. S. Lewis does not ask: "What needs to be done so humanity can live on?" Rather, the question is much less about how and much more about if. The author proposes the idea that all things have their time under the sun, and the we should rejoice in the time we have. To reinforce this idea, the book also expresses that "pleasure" and "remembrance" are not made separately; it states that if something brings you pleasure, you should try and retain that memory, so the simple thought of it can bring again the pleasure felt at the time.

One, however, cannot talk about "Out of the silent planet" without mentioning the philology in it. Ransom's discovery of the foreign language of the hrossa is a marvel in of itself. The author explores masterfully the complexities in learning a new language. It puts to proof the comment that many people who decide to move abroad say: "What you learn in six years in school, you learn in six months by simply being there". Being forcefully immersed in a world where you need to communicate to survive, but no one speaks your language, is perhaps the best representation of Ransom's journey to Malacandra. For me, at least, the icing on the cake was his lack of fluency by the end of the story; having the character master a totally unique language, that differs from every known language, would most certainly detract from the story's merits.

As anything in life, the book is not without its faults. I like to thing of them as mechanical faults during the writing process. One example is Ransom's watch: during his journey through the harandra to reach Oyarsa, Ransom is accompanied by one of the natives; when their travel reaches the destination and they part ways, Ransom tries to give the native his own watch; the native does not accept the gift, saying that it would be best given to another native. After that, however, Ransom's watch is not mentioned ever again. Mishaps such as this happen quite infrequently and do not subtract in any way from the story at large.

Overall, "Out of the silent planet" is a read that can be enjoyed and understood by a great audience. Although it evokes an exploration of the unknown, it is not overly abstract as was Arthur C. Clarke's "Rendevouz with Rama" and every alien aspect can be understood with ease. As mentioned, the religious aspect is very present, but are more philosophical than preachy. Even a reader with only little knowledge of religion should be able to understand the message. The book, hopefully, bring the reader to think about the future of the human race, the joys of life, the challenges in learning a new language, how one views their memories and the influence of evil in a society.
