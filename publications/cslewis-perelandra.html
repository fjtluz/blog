Perelandra

by C. S. Lewis

When C. S. Lewis stated in the foreword that "Perelandra" would be a sequel to "Out of the silent planet", and at the same time a standalone experience, I concede that, upon embarking on a new read, I did not fully understand what he meant. Now, as I have completed the Journey to Venus, I can safely affirm that, although "Perelandra" borrows many aspects from its predecessor, it is far from a continuation of the narative.

While the religious aspect of the prequel was vague and unintrusive, in "Perelandra", it is a central aspect of the narrative. The author brings great exposition to Christianity and the duality between good and evil; with parallels between Eden and the first sin. Perhaps in challenge, the author brings out false truths - misinterpretations of the gospel -, exploring the dichotomy between light and dark, between good and evil, between God and the Devil. But he does not try to hammer on this believes upon the reader, rather the contrary: he exposes them as possible truths, but then brings arguments that ultimately reveal their true light and confirm them to be nothing more than beautiful lies.

The first half of the story explores the "angel and devil on each shoulder" dynamic, but the second halve went in a direction I was not expecting. At the end, "this cannot go further" won against "something needs to be done". As a result the reader is left wandering what could have been. But perhaps this is the overall message, sometimes, things simply go the way they need to go.

For my taste, "Perelandra" greatest fault is its excess of reveries and descriptions, somewhat akin to J. R. R. Tolkien's style - a characteristic that was not at all present in the prequel. There are times when I felt out of the loop, not really understanding what the character was talking about, or how things would be relevant to the overarching story; in most cases, it felt like fodder, to eat up words and to fatten the pages. Nonetheless, I sincerely assume that it probably was my fault and that a second read might be warranted.

Overall, "Perelandra" is an enjoyable read. Although, I do think that I would require a second read in order to fully understand the message, but that will need to wait for another time. Now knowing it to be a very concrete standalone story, reading it with this in mind may help to fill the gaps and give me a better perspective on the nature of the story.
